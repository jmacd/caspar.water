            <h2 class="section-title">System design</h2>

            <p>In modern times, the Caspar Water System has been
              described as a "chlorinator in the woods". We use a
              relatively simple process to provide clean and safe
              drinking water to our community.</p>
	                
            <ul>
                <li><strong>Source:</strong> Our raw water is
                  sourced from a 188-feet deep well.</li>
                <li><strong>Disinfection:</strong> The water undergoes
                  chlorination to deactivate harmful bacteria and
                  waterborne pathogens.</li>
                <li><strong>Aeration:</strong> The water undergoes
                  aeration to raise pH and oxidize iron.
                <li><strong>Storage:</strong> Treated water is stored
                  in a 10,000-gallon concrete tank.</li>
		<li><strong>Distribution:</strong> The water main has a
		  linear layout with approximately 1 mile of pipe.
		  While it starts with six-inch pipe, maintenance during
		  our "ghost town" years has left the water main with a
		  mixture of materials and combination of 6", 4", 3",
		  and 2" pipe.
                <li><strong>Service:</strong> Our water system has 12
		  service connections, including the Caspar Community
		  Center and the historic Caspar Inn.
		</li>
                <li><strong>Pressure:</strong> Our water system
                  delivers water using gravity feed with static
                  pressures between 35psi and 60psi.
                </li>
            </ul>
	    
            <p>The aeration process works by removing carbon dioxide
              from the water through natural off-gassing. It's the
              reverse of the process causing ocean acidification,
              because the water has a higher concentration of carbon
              dioxide than the atmosphere. The addition of O₂ to the
              water disrupts the following chemical equilibrium:
	      
              <div class="science">
                <strong>CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻</strong><br>
		<strong>O₂ + HCO⁻ ⇌ HCO₃⁻</strong>
              </div>
	      
              As CO₂ is removed, fewer hydrogen ions (H⁺) are present,
              effectively raising the water's pH level. Our water is
              served with pH measuring around 6.8.
	    </p>

	    <p> In winter months, we serve approximately 800 gallons
	      per day. In summer months, we serve approximately 2,000
	      gallons per day.</p>
